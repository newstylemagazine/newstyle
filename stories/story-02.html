Reflections

Marcus Webb

Sometimes I catch my reflection in moments when I'm not prepared for it. A store window. The dark screen of my phone. The side mirror of a car. There's a fraction of a second where I don't recognize the face looking back, where it seems to belong to someone else entirely, someone I ought to have known better than to trust.

My father kept a mirror in his office that had belonged to his grandfather. It was gilt-framed and enormous, and it hung above a mantelpiece that held nothing but a few books and a plant that was probably dead already by the time I can remember. I asked him once why he needed such a large mirror, and he told me that it was important to see oneself clearly. I thought at the time that this was obvious, until I realized that people spend half their lives avoiding exactly that.

There's a park near my apartment where old men play chess in the summer. I walked past them last Tuesday and noticed that none of them were looking at the board. They were looking at their opponent's face. They were trying to read something there, some tells or tells-to-come, some invisible game being played beneath the visible one. I wondered if they ever won, and if they did, whether they felt satisfied, or whether satisfaction had learned to move in circuitous patterns too.

The window of my apartment faces north, which means I get light for most of the day, but it's never very warm. In winter, the cold comes through the glass like it's being sent on purpose, with some kind of instruction. I've gotten used to it now. There's a radiator that clanks and moans in the mornings, and I've stopped trying to adjust it. Some things, I've decided, are better left as they are. Some reflections are clearer when you don't look at them too hard.
