Untitled Poem

Elena Rousseau

The city at dusk is a watercolor running down the side of a building, all edges soft and uncertain. I walk through it as though I might disappear any moment, as though I am already half-dissolved. The people passing me do not look up. They are concentrated on their private trajectories, on destinations that matter only to them, and I admire this about them, this ability to believe that somewhere is worth arriving at.

In my grandmother's house there was a room she never entered. Forty years I came to visit, and never once did she open that door. I asked her why, and she said it was keeping something safe. I asked what, and she said the question was the reason she couldn't tell me. Before she died, she gave me a key. I have never used it. The house is not mine anymore, belongs to cousins I rarely speak to. They have probably changed that room entirely, or forgotten it was there.

There is a particular kind of silence that comes at three in the morning, after the city has decided it is done for a while. It is not an absence of sound so much as a sound in itself, a humming beneath everything, the engine room of the world running on its own. I sit with it sometimes and try to remember what it felt like to sleep through the night without knowing I was doing it. There was a time. There was.

Light will find you, no matter where you hide. Not to rescue you, but to make clear the things you wanted left in shadow. I think about this when I see someone crying on the train, their face illuminated by the window, and everyone else pretending not to see. I think about it in rooms where I cannot find the switch. I think about it in the space between one thought and the next, where all the light goes to hide before it comes looking for us again.
