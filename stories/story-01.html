The Morning After

Sarah Chen

The light came through the window in bars, each one casting a shadow across the hardwood floor that had not been swept in weeks. She stood in the kitchen with her hands on the counter, not looking at anything in particular, just feeling the cool laminate beneath her palms and remembering that this had once been someone's dream.

The coffee maker sat unplugged on the counter. She had bought it at a yard sale three years ago, convinced it would change something about her mornings, about the way she moved through the house. Instead, it had gathered dust in the cabinet under the sink until the plumbing started to leak and she had taken everything out, dried it all carefully, and decided it might as well stay on the counter. Why not, she thought. Why not.

Outside, the neighbors were taking out their trash. They had a daughter who had just started college somewhere upstate. Helen knew this because she had heard them talking about it in June, standing in their driveway where the light was good, speaking in the kind of fervent tones people usually reserved for prayer. She had wanted to tell them it would be fine, that their daughter would return changed but intact, that the world was both smaller and larger than they imagined. But she had said nothing, had only waved when they looked over at her, a small gesture that acknowledged her standing on her porch but little else.

The morning was advancing. In another hour or so, the light would move to a different part of the kitchen entirely, and the shadows would have shifted enough that someone looking in might think time had not passed at all or that it had passed very quickly, depending on their philosophy. Helen thought of philosophy briefly, of all the questions she had wanted to ask people but hadn't, long ago when she still believed that such questions mattered. Now she reached out and plugged in the coffee maker, just to hear the small mechanical sound it made when the heating element became live. It made no difference. Nothing changed. She turned it off again and went back to bed.
